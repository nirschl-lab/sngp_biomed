Uncertainty-Aware Image Classification in Biomedical Imaging Using SNGP

Uma Meleti*, Jeffrey J. Nirschl*,
University Of Wisconsin Madison
International Symposium on Biomedical Imaging 2026
*Indicates Equal Contribution
Paper Supplementary Code arXiv

Overview of the predictions of deterministic DNN vs SNGP on In-Distribution (blood cell) vs Out-of-Distribution (cardiac) data. The Spectral-Normalized Neural Gaussian Process (SNGP) provides robust uncertainty estimates, enabling reliable detection of distribution shift and improved model trustworthiness in clinical settings.

The critical importance of Out-of-Distribution detection in medical imaging. Medical AI systems occupy the high-risk region where both domain sensitivity and impact on patient outcomes are significant. A model trained to classify cats vs. dogs can afford mistakes, but a cancer prediction system must reliably detect when input data deviates from its training distribution. Without robust OOD detection, models risk making confidently incorrect predictions on unfamiliar data, potentially leading to misdiagnosis and patient harm.

Abstract

Accurate histopathologic interpretation is key for clinical decision-making; however, current deep learning models for digital pathology are often overconfident and poorly calibrated in out-of-distribution (OOD) settings, which limit trust and clinical adoption. Safety-critical medical imaging workflows benefit from intrinsic uncertainty-aware properties that can accurately reject OOD input. We implement the Spectral-normalized Neural Gaussian Process (SNGP), a set of lightweight modifications that apply spectral normalization and replace the final dense layer with a Gaussian process layer to improve single-model uncertainty estimation and OOD detection. We evaluate SNGP vs. deterministic and Monte Carlo dropout on six datasets across three biomedical classification tasks: white blood cells, amyloid plaques, and colorectal histopathology. SNGP has comparable in-distribution performance while significantly improving uncertainty estimation and OOD detection. Thus, SNGP or related models offer a useful framework for uncertainty-aware classification in digital pathology, supporting safe deployment and building trust with pathologists.

Class-logit entropy distributions reveal the uncertainty awareness of different models. Deterministic DNNs and MC Dropout assign low entropy to OOD samples, leading to overconfident predictions. SNGP, in contrast, assigns significantly higher entropy to OOD inputs, demonstrating superior uncertainty quantification and clear separation between in-distribution and out-of-distribution data.

ROC curves demonstrating OOD detection performance of Baseline, MC Dropout, and SNGP methods. SNGP achieves the highest AUC scores across all OOD datasets, showing superior uncertainty quantification and more reliable out-of-distribution detection compared to deterministic and MC Dropout baselines.

Datasets

Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) quantifies dataset similarity: lower values indicate greater semantic similarity, while higher values represent substantial domain shifts, ideal for evaluating OOD detection. We evaluate SNGP across three biomedical imaging tasks using six publicly available datasets: White blood cells (Acevedo 2020, Jung 2022), amyloid plaques (Tang 2019, Wong 2022), and colorectal histopathology (Kather 2016, 2018).
